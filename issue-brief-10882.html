Deep Dive · Issue #10882

Freehand Selection and Tap Selection Break on Tablet-Sized Screens

P2 — Medium-High Priority  ·  github.com/excalidraw/excalidraw/issues/10882 ↗

1. What Is Happening

Excalidraw offers a circle-selection tool — think of it like drawing a loop around objects on a table to pick them all up at once. You draw a freehand shape around the items you want, release, and they all get selected together.

On tablet-sized screens (or a browser window resized to tablet dimensions), this gesture misbehaves. After drawing your loop, the selection either disappears immediately, flickers, or the editing bar that lets you style the selected shapes fails to appear. It is as if you carefully circled a group of items on a table, but the moment you lifted your hand, everything scattered back to where it was.

Additionally, simply tapping on an individual shape in this tablet view can undo what the circle-selection just did — shapes that were grouped together get accidentally dropped from the selection when they should stay highlighted.

2. Who Is Affected

  • Tablet users — anyone accessing Excalidraw on an iPad, Android tablet, or a touchscreen laptop in tablet mode.
  • Desktop users with smaller or split browser windows — if a user snaps their browser to half the screen, the app automatically adjusts its layout to fit that size, triggering the same bug.
  • Teachers, presenters, and visual thinkers who rely on quickly grouping and styling multiple shapes at once are hit hardest, since the circle-selection tool is their primary way of selecting many shapes in one gesture.
This is not a rare edge case. Tablet usage of diagramming tools is very common, and working with the browser taking up only part of the screen is a daily habit for many laptop users.

3. Visual Description

Here is what a user experiences, step by step:

  1. Open Excalidraw on a tablet or resize the browser window to roughly half the screen width.
  2. Draw several shapes on the canvas — rectangles, arrows, text.
  3. Select the circle-selection tool (the freehand loop icon in the toolbar).
  4. Hold your finger or mouse button down and draw a loop around a group of shapes.
  5. Lift your finger or release the mouse button.
  6. Notice: the shapes you just circled are no longer highlighted — the selection vanishes, or the editing bar that should appear (letting you change color, size, etc.) does not show up.
  7. Try tapping a single shape instead — it selects correctly.
  8. Now try the circle-selection again — the previously tapped shape gets dropped from the selection unexpectedly.

The experience feels unreliable and unpredictable, making it hard to trust the tool.

4. Why It Matters

Picture a teacher leading a remote class. She is using her iPad to sketch a diagram — boxes, arrows, labels. She wants to recolor a whole section of the diagram at once, so she draws a loop around it with her finger. Nothing stays selected. She tries again. Still nothing. Thirty seconds pass. Her students are watching. She gives up and changes each shape one by one.

That moment of friction is not just an inconvenience — it is a quiet vote of no confidence in the tool. And it happens every time a tablet user tries to do something that should be natural and instant.

This matters for three concrete reasons:

  • Lost time adds up. Every failed selection means extra taps, extra retries, extra frustration. For users who work in Excalidraw for hours a day — teachers, designers, meeting facilitators — this is death by a thousand cuts.
  • Tablets are not a niche. iPad and Android tablet usage for creative and educational work has grown steadily. Many laptop users also trigger this bug without realizing it, simply by working with a half-screen browser window. This reaches far more people than just dedicated tablet users.
  • First impressions are lasting. Users who hit this bug early in their experience with Excalidraw often assume the tool is unreliable. They may not report it — they just quietly stop using it, or tell a colleague it "felt glitchy." That reputation is hard to recover.

5. Proposed Solution (Simplified)

The fix would ensure that the tablet view of Excalidraw respects the circle-selection the same way the desktop view does. Specifically:

  • When you finish drawing a loop on a tablet, the selected shapes stay selected — they do not disappear.
  • The editing bar appears reliably after a circle-selection, just like it does on desktop.
  • Tapping a single shape no longer accidentally clears what you just selected with the loop.

From a user's perspective: circle-selection would simply work the same way on a tablet as it does on a desktop computer.

6. Priority Recommendation

Medium-High Priority
  • This bug affects one of the most fundamental things a user can do — selecting shapes — on a device category that is growing, not shrinking.
  • The problem appeared when the circle-selection tool was first introduced, meaning it has been present from the start and a fix is well within reach.
  • The bug is easy to reproduce and well understood — it does not require extensive investigation, so the effort to fix it is low relative to how many users it affects.
  • Leaving it unfixed sends a signal that the tablet experience is second-class, which could affect adoption among educators and visual professionals who favor tablets.

7. Questions for the Development Team

Before approving this work, a Product Owner should get clear answers to the following:

1. Does fixing this for tablets risk breaking anything for desktop or phone users?

The circle-selection tool behaves differently depending on screen size, and the fix will need to change that behavior for tablets specifically. Understanding whether the change is safely contained — or whether it could unintentionally affect users on other devices — is essential before committing to the work.

2. How do we know when this is truly fixed? What does a passing test look like?

A clear definition of "done" matters here. The team should be able to describe a specific scenario — for example, "draw a loop around three shapes on a tablet screen, release, and all three remain selected with the style options visible" — that can be tested consistently before the fix goes live.

3. Are there other tools or interactions in the tablet view that share this same underlying problem?

If the tablet layout has a broader issue with how it handles selections, fixing just the circle-selection tool may leave similar problems in place elsewhere. Knowing whether this is an isolated bug or a symptom of something bigger will help the team decide whether a narrow fix is enough, or whether a wider review of the tablet experience is warranted.

8. Why Keeping This Issue Open Matters

Bugs like this one are more than just individual annoyances — they are signals about where the product needs to grow. Keeping issue #10882 open serves several important purposes:

  • It keeps the conversation alive. As more users encounter this bug on tablets and report similar experiences, the thread becomes a valuable collection of real-world feedback. Each comment adds context that helps the team understand how widespread the problem truly is.
  • It tracks progress honestly. Open issues are a transparent record of known limitations. Closing an issue prematurely — before the underlying problem is fully resolved — risks the bug coming back silently in a future update, with no trail to investigate from.
  • It invites community contributions. Excalidraw is free and built openly by volunteers and contributors around the world. Many of those contributors look for well-documented, focused bugs to work on. A clearly described issue is an open invitation for someone in the community to step in and help — at no cost to the core team.
  • It reflects a commitment to quality. Users who discover that their bug report is being actively tracked — rather than quietly closed — feel heard. That trust is foundational to a healthy community and to long-term loyalty to the product.

Excalidraw improves every day because its maintainers and contributors treat each reported issue as an opportunity to make the tool more reliable, more inclusive, and more delightful to use. Issue #10882 is exactly that kind of opportunity.
